    Contents

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 16 June 1862

    Anderson writes home to Louisa from Cincinnati, Ohio. He is hauling Coal Boat men from Louisville, Kentucky. While in Cincinnati, Anderson visits his mother-in-law (whom he calls the "old lady") as well as family friends. During the dry, dusty, June weather, a fire breaks out near the Ohio River and Third St, destroying several houses. Anderson closes the letter by inviting Louisa to accompany him on his next trip to Cincinnati.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 27 June 1862.

    Writing from Pittsburgh, Anderson tells Louisa that his boat's departure has been delayed by a day and asks her to send some food and clothes to him at the dock in Shousetown, PA. He also expresses anxiety about whether his trip will be profitable and asks his wife to request some money from a Mr. Russell to help cover an upcoming payday for his crew.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 11 July 1862

    Anderson writes that he hopes his boat will beat riverboat Fanny McBurney to Maysville, KY in order to realize more profit on the trip, though he thinks his "prospects are poor" and is concerned about delays due to low water. He complains that his sleep has been restless because of mosquitoes, and he asks Louisa to write more—and at greater length—about their children and their crops.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, July 28, 1862

    Anderson discusses details of his trading and shares reflections on the war, including a group of prisoners aboard his riverboat and his sense that the war is "far from over."

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 10 August 1862

    Anderson complains of low water on the river, reports on a visit to Louisa's mother, and assures her that he is unlikely to be drafted as long as he is "in part in the government employ" transporting soldiers and government freight.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife Mary Louisa Anderson on August 16, 1862

    In his letter of 16 August 1862, Anderson notes that a broken shaft and low water threatens his prospects of profiting from government trade and favorable prices. He also describes the scene at Gallipolis where he boarded soldiers bound for induction into the army at Camp Portsmouth: "it would have made the hardest heart melt to have heard the woman [sic] and children weeping on the wharf Boat."

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife Mary Louisa Anderson on August 24, 1862

    In his letter of 24 August 1862 from Cincinnati, Anderson reports that his boat has been repaired and he has considered but rejected several offers to buy it. He notes that he had to give a one thousand dollar "Bail" to leave Pennsylvania and had to promise to return "by the 3rd of next month if drafted." He describes the enthusiasm of recruits boarding the train at small stations all along his return route to Cincinnati, but he also notes the women and children bidding the recruits goodbye and lamenting the distress "that this war has brought on the human family."

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Mary Louisa Anderson, on September 1st, 1862 from Cincinnati, Ohio.

    In this document, William B. Anderson lists his itinerary and the business conducted at each port of call. He brings oats to Gallipolis, Ohio; retrieves wheat from Graham's Station, West Virginia, and brings it to Blennerhasset's Island, West Virginia. During his travels, it was necessary to build a chute and hire oxen in order to bring the wheat to Blennerhasset's Island. In the latter part of the letter, Anderson discusses his hope to sell his boat, as business is stopped now that the previous travels are finished. He finishes by sending his love to his wife and children.

    ¤ A homesick letter to Mary Louisa Anderson written from Cincinnati on Sept. 17, 1862, by William B. Anderson

    In this letter, Anderson shows a bit of emotion as he recounts his encounters with the war thus far. Anderson writes of the impoverished life he and his fellow shipmates live and becomes nostalgic over the simpler pleasures in life like butter and beef. Anderson asks his wife to pay a vist to John Russell in order to collect the money that Russell owes Anderson; Anderson fears that Russell will be slow to repay if his wife does not express a strong sense of need. Anderson closes the letter optimistically speaking of great times to come with his wife and children once he returns home.

    ¤ Letter from William B Anderson to his wife, Louisa, dated September 28, 1862.

    William B Anderson tells his wife how he must take soldiers to Augusta, Kentucky. Anderson reports that he is doing well and that he is happy to hear that his wife's mother is also doing well. He wants his wife to take care of herself and the children. He describes when he thinks he may be home and for his wife to take care of the house and the work that needs to be done.

    ¤ October 7, 1862 Letter from William B. Anderson Pertaining to Everyday Life During the Civil War

    In this letter, Anderson reports hearing shooting along the river. He references two captains who were put in jail for cowardice. He expresses concern that he does not know what his orders will be from one day to the next. He speaks of a funeral of a friend's son in Maysville, and worries that his wife is sick or mad because he has not heard from her. This letter was written in October, making it one of the latest in the season, possibly indicating unseasonably warm weather or extenuating circumstances.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 16 October 1862.

    Anderson tells his wife that his boat's departure has been delayed by a day and asks her to send some food and clothes to him at the dock. He also expresses anxiety about whether his trip will be profitable and asks his wife to request some money from [John?] Russell to help cover an upcoming payday for his crew.

    ¤ My Dear Wife: A Riverboat Captain's Missive to His Love

    On June 19th, 1863, riverboat captain William B. Anderson penned a letter to his wife from St. Louis, Missouri, in which he informs his spouse of matters concerning river travel, his salary, and a bout of illness that left him bedridden.

    ¤ A letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Mary Louisa, from July 1, 1863 written in Cincinnati

    In this letter, from July 1, 1863, William B. Anderson writes to his wife, Mary Louisa, from Cincinnati. Anderson tells his wife about the Government cargo he is shipping, and the hard work that goes along with handling the cargo in the summer heat. Anderson also gives Mary Louisa advice pertaining to family and financial affairs back home in Pittsburgh. He ends the letter as he does most, with affection for his wife and telling her to kiss "the little ones" for him.

    ¤ W.B. Anderson During The Burning of Lebanon During the Cival War

    In this letter W.B. Anderson gives us a description of the difficulty he has unloading a shipment because of the civil war. More importantly though Anderson gives us an insight in this letter to the burning of Lebanon during the civil war, from the stand point of a civilian in public enterprise during this time. Some of the impact that we are given insight to includes the organization of local troops and how it is holding back Anderson and his shipment.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 18 July 1863

    Anderson shares with his wife the status of his crew and currently what cargo he is carrying, though he does not share with her his destination, he promises to write to her from either Evansville or Nashville. He also tells her that he has prepared the finances and taxes for Russell and for her to get any sugar she would like from him. He asks her to kiss his children for him and wishes them well while he is gone. He also apologizes for mistakes in spelling and other errors.

    ¤ Letter from riverboat captain William B. Anderson to his wife at home in Pittsburgh, dated July 31, 1863, from Cincinnati.

    Anderson informs his wife that the sickly Elias will bring her some money when he returns to Pittsburgh. Anderson then describes his current employment transporting goods for the government. He hopes to make enough money over the summer so that he can stay at home for the winter. While apologizing for an apparent scarcity of letters, Anderson claims that he has written frequently, and speculates that the Morgan raid may have interfered with mail delivery.

    ¤ W. B. Anderson Letter--8/1/1863--A busy moment for family and business.

    Anderson shoots off a short letter announcing a dinner with his wife's mother and Ella (as yet an unknown relative or friend mentioned in several of Anderson's letters). He suggests that the boat (Glide II) will leave that afternoon from Cincinnati for Nashville with 200 mules aboard. He sends by Adams Express $3600 for business-related expenses (Adams Express was a very profitable delivery company started in the 1850s and had parallel systems of delivery in the south during the war (Pixley, 2002). and reminds his wife that he sent $60 for family expenses with the previous letter. In this one-page, hurried letter to his wife--which followed a longer letter just the day before--Anderson suggests many things about the times and activities on the riverboat. He mentions the transport of animals (200 mules) along with personal commitments: Anderson met his wife's mother and Ella (an unknown relative or friend) on the boat (Glide II) and was about to leave for dinner. Before the afternoon departure, he was also intent upon sending business funds to his wife and money for family expenses: All this in one page on lined notebook paper with little punctuation and a postscript anticipating a subsequent letter from Nashville.

    ¤ Letter from William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 14 August 1864.

    Anderson informs his wife that he has arrived in Louisville, KY. He reports that a canal was closed and tells his wife that he was forced to hire men to keep some tobacco safe and slept with it on a subsequent night incase it rained. Anderson reports the local weather and asks her to send him pictures of their children. He also advises her of a new address to send letters to.

    Cin June 16/62
    My Dear Wife

    I did not write to you from Louisville on account of our short stay there & thought we would beet [sic] the Mail up, we will leave here to night [sic] we had a good trip of Coal boat men up here from Louisvile [sic] but our prospects are poor for a trip of either Freight or Passenger [sic] up, though upon the whole we make a paying trip I will finish this half sheet just before we start & then I can tell you more

    We have laid over and will leave this Evening we are getting all we want I have just been down to see your mother1 she is not very well Today sends her respects Ella2 was at school I did not see her. There was a large fire down between Third St and The river yesterday just one squear [sic] above the old ladys [sic] and burnt a lot of Old Houses & other property we have had all our rooms but 3 in the ladies Cabin full since last night but we have not up till this time had a lady passenger on our boat the old lady says she would like to have you pay her a visit so if you think you would enjoy a trip I will offer you an invitation we will if no accident get up about Saturday & leave again about Tuesday to day [sic] it is all most [sic] suffocatingly hot and the dust is so thick that I cannot scarcely see the buildings along the wharf & I think we must surely have a gust soon I will stop for I expect to spend Sunday with you & the small ones

    tell Mary McNamee that WM was over with us last evening and that he is well we will leave about 4 or 5 PM this Tuesday Eveng

    Affectionately & Truly Your
    WBA

    Cin July 11/62
    Dear Wife

    I have but a moment to drop you a line we are about to leave here it is now 1 Oclock and we are anxious to leave ahead of the Fanny McBurny4 for by doing so we Can beat her to Maysville and by so doing will be benefitted Materially our prospects are poor & we can not count on making much this trip but are bound [to] Keep the Boat moving I Telegraphed t[o] Pitt today to see what the prospect of water was & have not got an Answer to my dispatch yet, I am extremely Anxious to get home and will go as soon as water & other circumstances will permit last night was the worst night I ever attempted to put in, I never saw the like of the mosquitoes I tried to Sleep in my room & Failed and then failed and tried in the recess at the stern and failed then also & then on the Boiler deck & from that to the hurricane deck & still failed and John & I started up town and got a good room at the spence house and slept from 1 to 5 oclock which is the best sleep I have had for a week. You would not think that the mosquitoes could be as large and plentifull [sic] as they are John says that they are worse than they were at California. Enough of Complaints I think you might string out your letters a little longer as you have a good and cool place to write and I have not, give my love to the children and yourself I am in a hurry Excuse bad writing

    Your Husband

    Cin Monday 28 1862
    Dear Wife

    Yours came safe to hand I was glad to hear of the welfare of the children and sorry to hear of your affection [sic] though hope you will soon be well. We had a very serious time getting down we got more frt than we wanted and stuck fast 36 Hours at the sisters 5 above Wheeting [sic] & had to lighten & then stuck again on Blanerh [sic] Isld for 12 Hours & had to lighten, & had fog & consequently did not get here till this Morning. I have entered into the Cin & Parkersburg Trade and will leave here for Parkersburg Tomorrow at 12 Oclock I have a full load engaged one item is 100 Beef Cattle for Kanatha [sic] 6 for a government office for which I get $300.no/00 and a full load of Flour Whisky [sic] &c. we had no accident to occur on our down trip that will amt. to anything serious though we wet a few Sacks of Oats. I will not go down to see the Old Lady to night [sic] as I want rest and if I feel not to [sic] much fatigued by morning I will go down while it is cool it has been exceedingly hot here today and I have been very bussily [sic] engaged and had scarcely any sleep since I left home so I require rest. Bill Whitacre came down to the Boat this evening to see me he looks very bad has a very long Beard. We had a short talk and he gives a dollfull [sic] account of of [sic] his experience south.

    There was some of thee [sic] hardest passengers down with us from Green twps brig this time I ever saw among the lot was 2 Prissoners [sic] in Irons on their way to Frankford [sic] Prison7 one for downing a man & the other for steeling [sic] sheep you would think that the very devil had broke loose if you would see and hear all that I do about the Sesseshinists [sic] and others the war is far from over I could have had a good trip to Nashville but did not like to go on account of the late news from down that way and now I think we are in the right place and have good prospects and will make money right fast we made a little on our down trip I will stop give all the children a kiss for me till [sic] Wm to be a good boy and take care of your own healh [sic]


    Affectionatle[y] your husband good night WBA


    Write to me again as soon as you get this and the letter will be here on our return I feel well though am almost worn out though think I will have it easier now as we will not stick so much in the Parkersbug [sic] trade WBA

    Cin Aug 10 /62
    My Dear Wife

    Your of 7 Came to hand today on our arrival & I was glad to hear of your health being better and that of the Children8 being good I have been well since I wrote to you though have suffered by heat Considerably we made 2 trips to Parkersburg & are bound up tomorrow again we have had uncommon hard work to get up have had to lighten at Buffington & Blennerhassetts Island & line flats & Barges and had a good ell of work and anxiety of mind for a little money we made between 3 and 400 Dolls last week I cannot tell what our   prospects will be this week yet but I hope we shall not lose anything

    I was down to see your mother this evening she & Ella are well I did not get down to see her last trip & it was as well for she had gone down to South Bend and did not get up till Tuesday she says that she enjoyed the trip and put up a large quantity of Blackberries Fruit of all kinds is very abundant and watter mellons [sic] are beginning to get plenty we had some to eat on the boat this evening that the crew bot [sic] which were very fine I wrote a letter to John Russell and requested him to pay my taxes I cannot tell you when you may look for me up as it is very uncertain when I can go up with the Boat & I want to make all I can out of her I will not leave her in any bodys hands untill compelled to by sickness or war I want you to try to make yourself as happy as as [sic] you Can under the circumstances I know how you feel about this war matter but you need not to make yourself uneasy on my account for you can rest assured that I will try to keep out of danger as well as I can I will be less likely to be drafted by being away than I would be if at home I am at present in part in the government employ in the way of a transport that is we carry a great many soldiers and large quantitys [sic] of Gov Frt. I expect if the water gets much lower we will not even [get] as far up as Parkersburg and may be employed to Carry Gov Frt. & soldiers up as far as Gallipolis & if such should be the case then I will be Free from the draft I will expect a letter here when I return which will be on this day week I will drop you a line tomorrow before we leave and let you know what our prospects are Kiss all the children for me and take care of yourself and them for me

    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson

    Cin Sunday 16th August 18629
    My DearWife

    As John is going home I take the opportunity of dropping you a few lines to inform you that I am well. we arrived here at noon today we have had bad luck since I wrote you last. we broke our shaft on our up trip and shall have to lay here for several days to have it mended or probably a new one made. I cannot learn what our luck will be in getting it repared [sic] till Morning I will probably get time to go up and see you while the reparing [sic] is going on if I can leave with any kind of propriety. it was one of the worst times that an accident could happen as the river is low and Government wants us and at good prices we were pressed into Gov Service at Gallipolis but were released on account of our broke shaft

    We brought down part of 3 Companies of Soldiers from Gallipolis to Camp Portsmouth and it would have made the hardest heart melt to have heard the woman [sic] and children weeping on the wharf Boat while the men were being ordered on board there was at least 70 women & 100 children there to take perhaps the last leave of their Husbands Brothers & Sons who have answered the call for men to try and restore peace and restore this once Glorious Country to peace. You see or Know nothing about this war or the effects produced upon families by it I am sick and tiard [sic] of solders [sic] and soldering [sic] we see nor hear but little else and what I am most fearfull of is that the end is not close at hand we carry every trip more or less sick and wounded men and any amount of woman [sic] on their way to look up their sons Husbands or Brothers while I am writing the wharf is covered from Broadway to Main street with fresh recruits from Indiana & Ohio on their way to the Little Miami RR Depot on their way to Camps to be uniformed I have not been down today to see your mother but will before I write again or go up to see you I will not get to more than just go up one day & return the next if I do go at al [sic] . Write to me soon and give me all the news and take care of yourself and all the children you need not be disappointed if I don’t go up though if I can leave at al[l] I will be sure to write to me as soon as you get this in care of Isham Fisher & Co Cin give my love to the children


    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson

    Cin Sunday Aug 24/62
    My Dr Wife

    I arrived here yesterday at 6 in the Morning & Found the crew all well and the Boat nearly repaired we are all ready for business and have a load engaged and will leave here tomorrow for Gallipolis and may if there is water go on up as far as Parkersburg and take the Freight that we left at Racine the time we broke our Shaft. I have had various opportunities of Chartering or selling our Boat but none that I would accept. we will stand a better chance on our return to sell or Charter if the river still continues to fall. Our expenses for repairs have been considerable though not as much as I had anticipated I caught the old lady & Ella in bed yesterday morning when I went in to see them. I went in and ran upstairs as I usually do and found the doors open and the old lady laying on the Sofa and Ella on the floor both sound asleep and the bed standing as it has stood since I first went down there while the Kenton was in the Memphis Trade. Though I suppose it is their choice and of cource [sic] we have no business to say a word, they are both well I promised to go down to day & see them and shall this evening. You ought to have seen me in Pittsburgh the day I left I had to get a pass to leave the state and give One Thousand Dollars Bail that I would return by the 3rd of next month if drafted. I don’t fear any such thing as being drafted though may be for there is no telling what there will be to do yet, the eccitement [sic] has abated to a great extent here since I left though all along the R Road the enthusaism [sic] is beyond bounds in the night as well as by day all the small stations that the Cars would stop at large quantities of recruits would flock aboard and an eaqually [sic] large quantity of woman [sic] and children would be there to bid Good bye, there is no telling the ammount [sic] of distres [sic] that this war has brought on the human family

    I will have to stop for I have no news of any importance [to] write about give my love to the children and watch Anna for I think that she is in need of close attention I was in great hopes to sell the boat but this far have been unsuccessfull I will expect you to write to me on the receipt of this. take good Care of yourself and the children


    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson


    PS the old lady got July letter
    WBA

    Sept 17th 1862
    My DearWife

    Yours Came to hand this morning & I was glad to hear from you and pleased to learn that the the children were well I am also well and am still employed in the same way as a dispatch Boat and our trips only extend down as far of late as Andersons Ferry 8 miles we made but two yesterday one at noon and one in the night all is quiet here now and the majority of the stores are open but there is nothing doing but what appertains to the war it is thought that there will be a Battle in Kentucky someplace soon but as it will not be in the vicinity of the River I will not get a chance of witnessing it I have heard so much of war and been so much among soldiers that I have lost all horrors that I used to entertain about war I would as soon go to war as not if I were stout and had no family for it has almost became a fashion to go all that are left out of the military ranks are old and decripped, and it seems as that it has became a necessity to turn out. I will never go unless compelled to and that in defence of my own property and for the preservation of the Union I am sure that the South will be completely broken up and the north greatly in debt though the debt will be amongst the north and the money will be left in the country and all will be benefitted more or less with it. over I have not seen your mother since I last wrote to you as I have to stay on the Boat10 I have no opportunity of seeing any body or any thing only soldiers guns and Cannons. We are almost tiard [sic] of this kind of work we live almost misserably [sic] poor I have not tasted any Butter for 4 weeks that I could stand and as for sauce we never have any thing of the kind we have Beef poor at that and Bakers Bred [sic] and the poorest Kind of vegatibles [sic] I ever saw all kinds of marketting is dur and poor the Country is as dry as a chip for all we have showers frequenty yesterday and last night and today is as warm a time as we have had this summer and the Mosquitoes are the worst I ever saw. I wish you would Call on John Russell and tell him you want some Money and keep all that you know to be good such as U.S. and all Pittsburgh Banks I fear that I shall fail to get all out of him if I don’t make frequent calls you must let on to him that you are in need or he will put you off. Tell all the children that I am not in war nor do I intend to go that I will come home when the river raises and that I have some things for them I have a fine lot of Peach stones and wattermellon [sic] seeds saved and I will go up town some of those days after bussiness [sic] resumes more fully and buy each of them some thing that will please them. I have thought frequently about our whereabouts and pleasures nine years ago, and how as you thought what may our position be nine year from now. I hope that we may have no more to mar our happiness than we have had and that we may both live and our children live many nine years to come. I will stop as my paper is full I will look for a letter from you on receipt of this and hope to hear of you and the little ones enjoying good health and happiness


    Ever Truly your husband
    WB Anderson

    CinAug 1st 1863
    DearWife

    As we leave this Evening I thot [sic] I would drop you a line to let you know that I have seen your mother she & Ella are on the Boat now & have been for an hour I have to go up town and they are waiting till I get ready we are to have 200 mules down after dinner & shall get out by 4 or 5 oclock I have no news to write about I send by Adams Express16 to Pitt today (3600. Three thousand six hundred Dolls to pay our 1st notes give my respects to all I am in a hurry and have not time write or think of any thing that will interest you give my respects to all I sent a letter last night by Elias and $60.00 in cash for you Kiss all the small ones for me good bye


    Yours affectionately
    WB Anderson


    P.S. I will write from Nashville
    again
    WBA

    Cin Aug 10 /64
    My DearWife

    We arrived here on Monday at 3 P.M. we had but Little trouble in getting down but Picked up nothing we were detaned [sic] by the Navy department with a view of making a Gun Boat of us until this morning & then rejected on [ap?] of me not agreeing with them on terms &c. now I have no alternative but to take a load to Louisville at low prices & then am required to report to the commissary department & expect to be sent to Tennesee River though have a hope of getting to come back to Cin on my own account if we are sent below I will write from Louisvill [sic] if not will write from here on my return I am well though very much out of heart not from any defects in the boat for she does well in every respect I would have written before now but wanted to wait till I knew our destination. I have not heard of the old Lady yet.

    It has been excessively hot ever since I left home and the mosquitoes are very bad I have not had any offer to sell the boat I will sell if any good Chance offers. I stopped at GallipolisGallipoli on our downward trip and proposed to put her in the Gallipols [sic] & Pitt trade in the fall & got the promise of all the patronage there which will be I think what I will do if I don’t sell I hope to hear from you on my return here. I have nothing of intrest to write about we will leave here tomorrow try & take good care of yourself and the children & get a girl as soon as you can for I know you need one I will close for my mind is not in tune to write long so good night for a while.


    affectionately your Husband
    WB Anderson


    5.P.M.
    P.S. Since writing the above Capt Good
    orders me out this evenig [sic]
    Your WBA

    Cin Sunday Aug 14 /64
    My DearWife

    Yours of 9th Came safe to hand I was pleased to hear from you. We arrived from Louisville on Friday Evening made a quick trip but very little Money when we got to Louisville the Canal was closed to repare and we came back with but 1/4 of a load on account of the R Road here having Telegraphed down to not ship up any more Frt as they were blocked full of Frt & could not take it in the Depots consequently could not take it off the wharf I put our Tobacco off Yesterday & Last night had to hire men & put it onboard the Boat today to Keep it safe for fear of rain now I have to lay here with it in the Boat till Tuesday & then I am to commence to load again for Louisville I have a little better prospect than before. I left you that Pittsbg Money & put it in that Big Pocket Book I Took the Gov money & Pd Mr Hamilton the 168$ to pay the Taxes with. I have not seen the Old Lady yet it has been Excessively hot today & has rained a little & is Cloudy with prospects of rain. I have been Tollerable well since I wrote last. I have no news of any importance to write about I would like you to send me one of Each of the Likenesses of You & the children to look at occasionally I may go below from Louisville this trip if the Canal is open though it is very uncertain

    Direct your letters to me in Care of Harry Davidson and co Cincinnati Ohio instead of Barker Hart & Cook & I will get them in the night as well as day for they keep a man to sleep in the store Kiss all the little ones and tell them to be good

    let me hear often from you & tell me how the crops look &c. &c. get a girl soon as you can & take care of your own health & that of the children


    Your affectionate Husband
    WB Anderson


    P.S. I will drop you a line again
    when I leave here
    Yours WBA

    6.  Anderson probably meant to write "Kanawha," referring either to Kanawha City, WV on the Kanawha River, which flows into the Ohio at Point Pleasant, OH between Cincinnati and Parkersburg, or the Little Kanawha River, which flows into the Ohio at Parkersburg. [Back]

    8. At the time this letter was written, W.B. Anderson and his wife Lousia had four children: William Y., Anna M., John L., and Robert(298-299). [Back]

    9. Anderson records the day as Sunday, but in August 1862, the 16th fell on a Saturday. [Back]

(Young?), age seven or eight; Anna Margaret, age six; John L., age four; and Robert Edward, age two. A fifth child, Harry C., would be born in January 1865. Tragically, William Y., Robert Edward, and Harry C. would all die of typhoid fever in 1890 (Robert Edward and Harry C. just a day apart), while Anna Margaret and John L. survived the disease that year. In a 2005 report, the Water Science and Technology Board states that Pittsburgh had the highest national typhoid death rate for large cities in the years 1873-1907; before the establishment of an effective water filtration system in 1907, the city drew its drinking water directly from the polluted Allegheny River.(Danker; Regional Cooperation, 36–37.)
