    Contents

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 16 June 1862

    Anderson writes home to Louisa from Cincinnati, Ohio. He is hauling Coal Boat men from Louisville, Kentucky. While in Cincinnati, Anderson visits his mother-in-law (whom he calls the "old lady") as well as family friends. During the dry, dusty, June weather, a fire breaks out near the Ohio River and Third St, destroying several houses. Anderson closes the letter by inviting Louisa to accompany him on his next trip to Cincinnati.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 27 June 1862

    Writing from Pittsburgh, Anderson tells Louisa that his boat's departure has been delayed by a day and asks her to send some food and clothes to him at the dock in Shousetown, PA. He also expresses anxiety about whether his trip will be profitable and asks his wife to request some money from a Mr. Russell to help cover an upcoming payday for his crew.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 11 July 1862

    Anderson writes that he hopes his boat will beat riverboat Fanny McBurney to Maysville, KY in order to realize more profit on the trip, though he thinks his "prospects are poor" and is concerned about delays due to low water. He complains that his sleep has been restless because of mosquitoes, and he asks Louisa to write more—and at greater length—about their children and their crops.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 28 July 1862

    Anderson discusses details of his trading and shares reflections on the war, including a group of prisoners aboard his riverboat and his sense that the war is "far from over."

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 10 August 1862

    Anderson complains of low water on the river, reports on a visit to Louisa's mother, and assures her that he is unlikely to be drafted as long as he is "in part in the government employ" transporting soldiers and government freight.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 16 August 1862

    In his letter of 16 August 1862, Anderson notes that a broken shaft and low water threatens his prospects of profiting from government trade and favorable prices. He also describes the scene at Gallipolis where he boarded soldiers bound for induction into the army at Camp Portsmouth: "it would have made the hardest heart melt to have heard the woman [sic] and children weeping on the wharf Boat."

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 24 August 1862

    In his letter of 24 August 1862 from Cincinnati, Anderson reports that his boat has been repaired and he has considered but rejected several offers to buy it. He notes that he had to give a one thousand dollar "Bail" to leave Pennsylvania and had to promise to return "by the 3rd of next month if drafted." He describes the enthusiasm of recruits boarding the train at small stations all along his return route to Cincinnati, but he also notes the women and children bidding the recruits goodbye and lamenting the distress "that this war has brought on the human family."

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 1 September 1862

    In his short letter of 1 September 1862, Anderson recounts more problems with low water, including the necessity of building a chute to move a load of wheat past Blennerhasset's Island. He also notes that he is still trying to sell his boat, but complains that business in Cincinnati is "stopped."

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 17 September 1862

    Anderson notes that a battle is expected soon in Kentucky. He writes that he has "lost all horrors" that he had about war and would enlist if he "were stout" and had no family, yet at the same time stresses that he "will never go unless compelled" and would do so primarily "in defence of my own property and for the preservation of the Union." He also describes the hardships of life on the river and muses about a happy life with his family after the war.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 28 September 1862

    Anderson tells his wife that he has been ordered to take soldiers to Augusta, KY in support of the Union Army's response to the Battle of Augusta the previous day. He notes that he has finally sold his boat and offers some business advice regarding the home farm. Apparently in response to Louisa's claim that he is "indifferent about home," he writes that he longs "to stay there the Bal of my days."

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 7 October 1862

    In his letter of 7 October 1862, Anderson reports on various changes affecting all of the boats in government service, and mentions the jailing of two riverboat captains for cowardice. Turning to family matters, he notes that he attended a funeral in Maysville for a friend's son, which affected him deeply.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 16 October 1862

    Anderson's letter of 10 October 1862 focuses on arrangements for a visit from his wife and on business matters, both at home and on the river.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 19 June 1863

    Anderson writes about business prospects and recounts a recent illness.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, on 1 July 1863

    Anderson writes about the government cargo he is shipping—potatoes and clothing—and the hard work involved in handling the cargo in the summer heat. He also advises Louisa about financial affairs back home in Pittsburgh.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, on 5 July 1863

    Anderson writes that "a rioting spree" by Irish workers in Cincinnati has disrupted work on the wharves and that "the city was all on uproar" over reports that Morgan's Raiders had "Burned down Lebanon" Kentucky. Anderson is skeptical of calls to send men to Kentucky to protect Lexington and Louisville, believing that the real purpose of the call up is "to get recruits and then . . . march them on to Richmond."

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 18 July 1863

    In this short letter, Anderson shares details about the health of a crew member, describes his current cargo of Sutlers' goods, scolds Louisa for not writing, and alerts her to some business arrangments.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 31 July 1863

    Anderson informs his wife that the sickly Elias will bring her some money when he returns to Pittsburgh. Anderson then describes his current employment transporting goods for the government. He hopes to make enough money over the summer so that he can stay at home for the winter. While apologizing for an apparent scarcity of letters, Anderson claims that he has written frequently, and speculates that the Morgan raid may have interfered with mail delivery.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 1 August 1863

    In his brief letter of 1 August 1863, Anderson shares some family and business news—a visit to the boat by Louisa's mother, a shipment of 200 mules, $3,600 sent by express to Pittsburgh to cover outstanding notes.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 10 August 1864

    Anderson writes about negotiations the government "with a view of making a Gun Boat of us"—a proposal that falls through because they could not agree on terms—as well as various other details of his shipping business.

    ¤ Letter from Anderson to his wife, Louisa, 14 August 1864

    Anderson informs his wife that he has arrived in Cincinnati from Louisville. He reports that a canal in Louisville was closed, resulting in various problems, from having to come back to Cincinnati with "but 1/4 of a load," then having to offload a shipment of tobacco only to reload it to protect it from rain. In addition to relating various business matters, Anderson asks Louisa to send "Likenesses of You & the children to look at occasionally."

    Cin June 16/62
    My Dear Wife

    I did not write to you from Louisville on account of our short stay there & thought we would beet [sic] the Mail up, we will leave here to night [sic] we had a good trip of Coal boat men up here from Louisvile [sic] but our prospects are poor for a trip of either Freight or Passenger [sic] up, though upon the whole we make a paying trip I will finish this half sheet just before we start & then I can tell you more

    We have laid over and will leave this Evening we are getting all we want I have just been down to see your mother1 she is not very well Today sends her respects Ella2 was at school I did not see her. There was a large fire down between Third St and The river yesterday just one squear [sic] above the old ladys [sic] and burnt a lot of Old Houses & other property we have had all our rooms but 3 in the ladies Cabin full since last night but we have not up till this time had a lady passenger on our boat3 the old lady says she would like to have you pay her a visit so if you think you would enjoy a trip I will offer you an invitation we will if no accident get up about Saturday & leave again about Tuesday to day [sic] it is all most [sic] suffocatingly hot and the dust is so thick that I cannot scarcely see the buildings along the wharf & I think we must surely have a gust soon I will stop for I expect to spend Sunday with you & the small ones4

    tell Mary McNamee that WM was over with us last evening and that he is well we will leave about 4 or 5 PM this Tuesday Eveng

    Affectionately & Truly Your
    WBA

    Cin July 11/62
    Dear Wife

    I have but a moment to drop you a line we are about to leave here it is now 1 Oclock and we are anxious to leave ahead of the Fanny McBurny6 for by doing so we Can beat her to Maysville and by so doing will be benefitted Materially our prospects are poor & we can not count on making much this trip but are bound [to] Keep the Boat moving I Telegraphed t[o] Pitt today to see what the prospect of water was & have not got an Answer to my dispatch yet, I am extremely Anxious to get home and will go as soon as water & other circumstances will permit last night was the worst night I ever attempted to put in, I never saw the like of the mosquitoes I tried to Sleep in my room & Failed and then failed and tried in the recess at the stern and failed then also & then on the Boiler deck & from that to the hurricane deck & still failed and John & I started up town and got a good room at the spence house and slept from 1 to 5 oclock which is the best sleep I have had for a week. You would not think that the mosquitoes could be as large and plentifull [sic] as they are John says that they are worse than they were at California. Enough of Complaints I think you might string out your letters a little longer as you have a good and cool place to write and I have not, give my love to the children and yourself I am in a hurry Excuse bad writing

    Your Husband
    WB Anderson

    Your respectfuly [sic]
    WB Anderson

    PS be sure to write as before I enclosed an Envellop [sic] in Yesterdays letter


    WBA

    Cin Monday 28 18627
    Dear Wife

    Yours came safe to hand I was glad to hear of the welfare of the children and sorry to hear of your affection [sic] though hope you will soon be well. We had a very serious time getting down we got more frt than we wanted and stuck fast 36 Hours at the sisters 8 above Wheeting [sic] & had to lighten & then stuck again on Blanerh [sic] Isld for 12 Hours & had to lighten, & had fog & consequently did not get here till this Morning. I have entered into the Cin & Parkersburg Trade and will leave here for Parkersburg Tomorrow at 12 Oclock I have a full load engaged one item is 100 Beef Cattle for Kanatha [sic] 9 for a government office for which I get $300.no/00 and a full load of Flour Whisky [sic] &c. we had no accident to occur on our down trip that will amt. to anything serious though we wet a few Sacks of Oats. I will not go down to see the Old Lady to night [sic] as I want rest and if I feel not to [sic] much fatigued by morning I will go down while it is cool it has been exceedingly hot here today and I have been very bussily [sic] engaged and had scarcely any sleep since I left home so I require rest. Bill Whitacre came down to the Boat this evening to see me he looks very bad has a very long Beard. We had a short talk and he gives a dollfull [sic] account of of [sic] his experience south.

    There was some of thee [sic] hardest passengers down with us from Green twps brig this time I ever saw among the lot was 2 Prissoners [sic] in Irons on their way to Frankford [sic] Prison10 one for downing a man & the other for steeling [sic] sheep you would think that the very devil had broke loose if you would see and hear all that I do about the Sesseshinists [sic] and others the war is far from over I could have had a good trip to Nashville but did not like to go on account of the late news from down that way and now I think we are in the right place and have good prospects and will make money right fast we made a little on our down trip I will stop give all the children a kiss for me till [sic] Wm to be a good boy and take care of your own healh [sic]


    Affectionatle[y] your husband good night WBA

    Write to me again as soon as you get this and the letter will be here on our return I feel well though am almost worn out though think I will have it easier now as we will not stick so much in the Parkersbug [sic] trade WBA

    Cin Aug 10 /62
    My Dear Wife

    Your of 7 Came to hand today on our arrival & I was glad to hear of your health being better and that of the Children being good I have been well since I wrote to you though have suffered by heat Considerably we made 2 trips to Parkersburg & are bound up tomorrow again we have had uncommon hard work to get up have had to lighten at Buffington & Blennerhassetts Island & line flats & Barges and had a good ell of work and anxiety of mind for a little money we made between 3 and 400 Dolls last week I cannot tell what our   prospects will be this week yet but I hope we shall not lose anything

    I was down to see your mother this evening she & Ella are well I did not get down to see her last trip & it was as well for she had gone down to South Bend and did not get up till Tuesday she says that she enjoyed the trip and put up a large quantity of Blackberries Fruit of all kinds is very abundant and watter mellons [sic] are beginning to get plenty we had some to eat on the boat this evening that the crew bot [sic] which were very fine I wrote a letter to John Russell and requested him to pay my taxes I cannot tell you when you may look for me up as it is very uncertain when I can go up with the Boat & I want to make all I can out of her I will not leave her in any bodys hands untill compelled to by sickness or war I want you to try to make yourself as happy as as [sic] you Can under the circumstances I know how you feel about this war matter but you need not to make yourself uneasy on my account for you can rest assured that I will try to keep out of danger as well as I can I will be less likely to be drafted by being away than I would be if at home I am at present in part in the government employ in the way of a transport that is we carry a great many soldiers and large quantitys [sic] of Gov Frt. I expect if the water gets much lower we will not even [get] as far up as Parkersburg and may be employed to Carry Gov Frt. & soldiers up as far as Gallipolis & if such should be the case then I will be Free from the draft I will expect a letter here when I return which will be on this day week I will drop you a line tomorrow before we leave and let you know what our prospects are Kiss all the children for me and take care of yourself and them for me

    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson

    Cin Sunday 16th August 186211
    My DearWife

    As John is going home I take the opportunity of dropping you a few lines to inform you that I am well. we arrived here at noon today we have had bad luck since I wrote you last. we broke our shaft on our up trip and shall have to lay here for several days to have it mended or probably a new one made. I cannot learn what our luck will be in getting it repared [sic] till Morning I will probably get time to go up and see you while the reparing [sic] is going on if I can leave with any kind of propriety. it was one of the worst times that an accident could happen as the river is low and Government wants us and at good prices we were pressed into Gov Service at Gallipolis but were released on account of our broke shaft

    We brought down part of 3 Companies of Soldiers from Gallipolis to Camp Portsmouth and it would have made the hardest heart melt to have heard the woman [sic] and children weeping on the wharf Boat while the men were being ordered on board there was at least 70 women & 100 children there to take perhaps the last leave of their Husbands Brothers & Sons who have answered the call for men to try and restore peace and restore this once Glorious Country to peace. You see or Know nothing about this war or the effects produced upon families by it I am sick and tiard [sic] of solders [sic] and soldering [sic] we see nor hear but little else and what I am most fearfull of is that the end is not close at hand we carry every trip more or less sick and wounded men and any amount of woman [sic] on their way to look up their sons Husbands or Brothers while I am writing the wharf is covered from Broadway to Main street with fresh recruits from Indiana & Ohio on their way to the Little Miami RR Depot on their way to Camps to be uniformed I have not been down today to see your mother but will before I write again or go up to see you I will not get to more than just go up one day & return the next if I do go at al [sic] . Write to me soon and give me all the news and take care of yourself and all the children you need not be disappointed if I don’t go up though if I can leave at al[l] I will be sure to write to me as soon as you get this in care of Isham Fisher & Co Cin give my love to the children


    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson

    Cin Sunday Aug 24/62
    My Dr Wife

    I arrived here yesterday at 6 in the Morning & Found the crew all well and the Boat nearly repaired we are all ready for business and have a load engaged and will leave here tomorrow for Gallipolis and may if there is water go on up as far as Parkersburg and take the Freight that we left at Racine the time we broke our Shaft. I have had various opportunities of Chartering or selling our Boat but none that I would accept. we will stand a better chance on our return to sell or Charter if the river still continues to fall. Our expenses for repairs have been considerable though not as much as I had anticipated I caught the old lady & Ella in bed yesterday morning when I went in to see them. I went in and ran upstairs as I usually do and found the doors open and the old lady laying on the Sofa and Ella on the floor both sound asleep and the bed standing as it has stood since I first went down there while the Kenton was in the Memphis Trade. Though I suppose it is their choice and of cource [sic] we have no business to say a word, they are both well I promised to go down to day & see them and shall this evening. You ought to have seen me in Pittsburgh the day I left I had to get a pass to leave the state and give One Thousand Dollars Bail that I would return by the 3rd of next month if drafted. I don’t fear any such thing as being drafted though may be for there is no telling what there will be to do yet, the eccitement [sic] has abated to a great extent here since I left though all along the R Road the enthusaism [sic] is beyond bounds in the night as well as by day all the small stations that the Cars would stop at large quantities of recruits would flock aboard and an eaqually [sic] large quantity of woman [sic] and children would be there to bid Good bye, there is no telling the ammount [sic] of distres [sic] that this war has brought on the human family

    I will have to stop for I have no news of any importance [to] write about give my love to the children and watch Anna for I think that she is in need of close attention I was in great hopes to sell the boat but this far have been unsuccessfull I will expect you to write to me on the receipt of this. take good Care of yourself and the children


    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson

    PS the old lady got July letter WBA

    WB Anderson

    Cin Sept 17th 1862
    My Dear Wife

    Yours Came to hand this morning & I was glad to hear from you and pleased to learn that the the children were well I am also well and am still employed in the same way as a dispatch Boat and our trips only extend down as far of late as Andersons Ferry 8 miles we made but two yesterday one at noon and one in the night all is quiet here now and the majority of the stores are open but there is nothing doing but what appertains to the war it is thought that there will be a Battle in Kentucky someplace soon but as it will not be in the vicinity of the River I will not get a chance of witnessing it I have heard so much of war and been so much among soldiers that I have lost all horrors that I used to entertain about war I would as soon go to war as not if I were stout and had no family for it has almost became a fashion to go all that are left out of the military ranks are old and decripped [sic] , and it seems as that it has became [sic] a necessity to turn out. I will never go unless compelled to and that in defence of my own property and for the preservation of the Union I am sure that the South will be completely broken up and the north greatly in debt though the debt will be amongst the north and the money will be left in the country and all will be benefitted more or less with it. over I have not seen your mother since I last wrote to you as I have to stay on the Boat I have no opportunity of seeing any body or any thing only soldiers guns and Cannons. We are almost tiard [sic] of this kind of work we live almost misserably [sic] poor I have not tasted any Butter for 4 weeks that I could stand and as for sauce we never have any thing of the kind we have Beef poor at that and Bakers Bred [sic] and the poorest Kind of vegatibles [sic] I ever saw all kinds of marketting [sic] is due and poor the Country is as dry as a chip for all we have showers frequenty yesterday and last night and today is as warm a time as we have had this summer and the mosquitoes are the worst I ever saw. I wish you would Call on John Russell and tell him you want some Money and keep all that you know to be good such as U.S. and all Pittsburgh Banks I fear that I shall fail to get all out of him if I don’t make frequent calls you must let on to him that you are in need or he will put you off. Tell all the children that I am not in war nor do I intend to go that I will come home when the river raises and that I have some things for them I have a fine lot of Peach stones and wattermellon [sic] seeds saved and I will go up town some of those days after bussiness [sic] resumes more fully and buy each of them some thing that will please them. I have thought frequently about our whereabouts and pleasures nine years ago, and how as you thought what may our position be nine year from now. I hope that we may have no more to mar our happiness than we have had and that we may both live and our children live many nine years to come. I will stop as my paper is full I will look for a letter from you on receipt of this and hope to hear of you and the little ones enjoying good health and happiness


    Ever Truly your husband WB Anderson

    Cin Oct 7th 1862
    My Dear Wife

    As I have found out what we are to do for a little while I let you know this morning we were ordered to go below the Bridge and go in our old trade that is to go in the dispatch bussiness [sic] and we still carry our Gun Squad of Eleven Men we have just made a trip down to the J B Gurly at Andersons ferry & back & found all well on board though a little excitement down that way Some shooting was heard back in the country and a dispatcher sent out to asscertain [sic] the cause and I look for an answer back such as has occurred lots of times before that there was a review and shoo of Blank cartridges by way of stimulating the Men at hearing the cracks &c. I have good reason to expect that we will be soon discharge as I have heard that all Boats in Gov service will be dismissed this week unless there should be an attack made by the rebels I find that the Comadore is dissatisfied at some of the boats engaged in the flottilla and the intention is to call all in and dismiss all, and such as are absolutely needed he will renengage [sic] again. I feel safe that we shall fare well and if there is any money to be got out of the chartering bussiness [sic] we will fare too You may think that this is a strange sort of life and so do I! I cannot tell today what I shall do tomorrow nor can a I find out by enquiry as the Comadore [sic] of the fleet is subject to the orders of Gen Wright. I suppose you heard by the papers of the cowardice of Capt Adams and the Capt of the Florence Miller who are both in Jail and likely to be Kept there I don’t Know what will be their doom yet.

    I have no news of any interest to write to you I have not seen your mother since last Saturday week she was well I have not had any letter from you for over a week and it worries me are you sick or are you mad or are any of the children sick let me hear from you often. I witnessed a funeral persession [sic] on last Saturday of one of James Loves children at Maysville and it struck me sorely to see it as he and us were both married on the Same day and have had the same number of children and they lack but a little of being of the same ages and he lost the youngest one a boy of the same size and near the same age of Eddie it made me feel very queer indeed as I had not heard from you when at Cin last I wish you was here or I Could go up and stay at home a while but that is out of the question. Kiss all the children for me and tell them to be good that I will soon Come home take good care of yourself good bye

    Your affectionate husband WBA

    Cin October 16th 1862
    My Dr Wife

    Yours Mailed 13th I got on my arrival to the City this afternoon & was glad to hear from you. I have been absent from the City for a few days on a trip to Maysville we are still in Gov employ and I suppose we will continue some time yet as there is no prospect of water. You can make your own arrangements about the children as I leave the care of them altogether to you I think if you will come down you had better not wait as long as next month for fear the river will raise or if it dont [sic] raise & should continue to fall we might be sent down below. this my advise [sic] to you will be for you to come by the Steubenville route and leave Pittsburgh at one Oclock or there abouts and when you get on board you tell the Conductor that you want to take the sheeping [sic] Car which they take in tow at Steubenville and that will cost you 50 cts extra that train arrives at the Dayton & Hamilton Depot at about 5 or 6 oclock A.M. the ticket office is on Liberty Street near the Canal on the left hand side of the street it might be that John will come down with you and that would be better as I think he has acted very foolish for I have to pay 5$ each per day for Pilots and he might just as well had 200$ dollars while he has been at home as not if you should come down or have any chance to send me that pair of Blue Pants & belt down I would like to have them as my Pants are getting thin and I don’t want to spend any money I can help. I have not seen your mother for 2 or three weeks I may see her in a few days if you come let me know a few days before you come when to be on the look out for you.

    Tell Old Man Walsh that I will take 12 dolls for that Heifer & for him to sell the Hay and divide the money I think hay will be very High in the spring but I suppose he wants his share of the money he can do as he thinks best about it. That Bank notice you spoke about I made arrangements with Holmes & Sons Brokers to pay all my notes that will fall due I told Mr Wright to use what Money was deposited at Holmes & Sons belonging to the Boat to Pay Notes & when that ran out to use what I had there which is sufficient to pay all claims that may fall due for a while I will look for a letter from you soon and will be pleased to hear of you making satisfactory arrangements to come and see me as it seems utterly impossibly for me to leave for the present I am to settle up our business here up till the 15 of this Mo this or next week and then I will find out what I am making but one thing I do know is this that I am doing as well as I want to with a boat my share of the proffits [sic] and wages is at least 40 dolls per day and that is not bad but keep it to yourself. I will stop as the mail Closes at 8 Oclock this evening I will write soon again but write immediately any [sic] give me all the news and take good Care of yourself and the children and kiss all of them for me and tell them to be good


    Your Affectionate Husband
    WB Anderson

    Excuse Haste & Bad writing as there is a constant talking and fun in the Hall


    WBA

    St Louis Saturday June 19th 6312
    My Dear Wife

    I will commence a letter to you this afternoon & Finish it tomorrow we arrived here last night about Midnight we had a fair trip around & if we get as good a one back we will make a little money times are very dull & and prices poor we had all the freight we could carry on the water I have several small lots engaged to go back to Louisville & Cin. & I think by Monday night we shall have all we can carry I had thought somebody would be here and offer to buy the boat but the Boat fervor has subsided greatly I had a visit from Capt R C Gray today & he says that he thinks that the upper Mississippi river trade would pay us better than the St Louis and Cin. trade but I dont wish to go up there for the present. I was not well one minute at a time until I took the medicine and it acted like a charm I am of course weak but feel well and tomorrow I want to go and see Belle Ferguson I have not been out of my room nor in fact out of Bed all day till this Evening. I would not have believed that a few doses of medicine would do so much in so short a time. I will send you a paper or two tomorrow I hope you are well give my love to the children excuse this poor letter for I can assure you that I have had letter [sic] to say – I will leave here for Cin tomorrow night without I get disappointed. I have been unable to go up town to see how it looks but will tomorrow if nothing happens & my health improves I feell [sic] sure I will be all right again in a day or two


    Yours Affectionately
    WB Anderson

    P.S. I will write again tomorrow any how

    WBA

    Cin Sunday Afternoon14
    Dear Wife

    Your affectionate husband
    WB Anderson

    My Dear Wife

    Yours of 9th Came safe to hand I was pleased to hear from you. We arrived from Louisville on Friday Evening made a quick trip but very little Money when we got to Louisville the Canal was closed to repare [sic] and we came back with but 1/4 of a load on account of the R Road here having Telegraphed down to not Ship up any more Frt as they were blocked full of Frt & could not take it in the Depots consequently could not take it off the wharf I put our Tobacco off Yesterday & Last night had to hire men & put it onboard the Boat today to Keep it safe for fear of rain now I have to lay here with it in the Boat till Tuesday & then I am to commence to load again for Louisville I have a little better prospect than before. I left you that Pittsbg Money & put it in that Big Pocket Book I Took the Gov money & Pd Mr Hamilton the 168$ to pay the Taxes with. I have not seen the Old Lady yet it has been Excessively hot today & has rained a little & is Cloudy with prospects of rain. I have been Tollerable well since I wrote last. I have no news of any importance to write about I would like you to send me one of Each of the Likenesses of You & the children to look at occasionally I may go below from Louisville this trip if the Canal is open though it is very uncertain

    Direct your letters to me in Care of Harry Davidson and co Cincinnati Ohio instead of Barker Hart & Cook & I will get them in the night as well as day for they keep a man to sleep in the store Kiss all the little ones and tell them to be good

    let me hear often from you & tell me how the crops look &c. &c. get a girl as soon as you can & take care of your own health & that of the children


    Your affectionate Husband
    WB Anderson

    P.S. I will drop you a line again when I leave here Yours WBA

    MS Description. Anderson's letter of 14 August 1864 from Cincinnati is written in ink on two sides of a loose sheet of manilla-colored paper measuring approximately 7.5 inches wide by 9.0 inches tall (19 x 23 cm) and ruled on the recto side with horizontal blue lines spaced .33 inches (0.8 cm) apart, except for a 1.5-inch (3.8 cm) unruled top margin; the verso is unlined. The left edge of the sheet is ragged, while the other three edges are clean, suggesting that the sheet was torn from a notepad or bound notebook, or a larger sheet. It appears originally to have been folded once crosswise and twice lengthwise. An indistinct, embossed watermark appears in the upper left-hand corner of the lined side of the sheet.

    Explanatory Annotations

    1. Mary Louisa Anderson's mother, Ann (Woolslayer) Fischgens (History of Allegheny County 298). [Back]

    2. Ella Jane Fis was Louisa's younger sister, born in 1851. She died in Cincinnati on September 4, 1863 (Danker, William B. Anderson Letters). [Back]

    3. Perhaps the Glide, a river steamer which Anderson eventually sold to the U.S. government (Jordan 506). [Back]

    4. According to information from Anderson descendent Jamie Danker, at the time this letter was written the Andersons had four young children: William Y., born 1855; Anna Margaret, born 1857; John L., born 1859; and Robert Edward, born 1861. A fifth child, Harry C., would be born in January 1865. William, Robert, and Harry would all die of typhoid fever in 1890 (Robert and Harry just a day apart), while Anna Margaret and John survived the disease that year. In a 2005 report, the Water Science and Technology Board states that Pittsburgh had the highest national typhoid death rate for large cities in the years 1873-1907; before the establishment of an effective water filtration system in 1907, the city drew its drinking water directly from the polluted Allegheny River (Danker, "Re: Request for permission"; Regional Cooperation, 36–37 ). [Back]

    5.  This pencilled note appears in the top margin. It was presumably added after the letter was composed, but the context is unknown. [Back]

    6. Presumably the Fanny McBurney (Fanny McBurnie), a Union stern-wheeled steamer built at Cincinnati in 1860 (Gaines 94; History of Washington County 379). [Back]

    7.  While Anderson does not include the month in his dateline, July was the only month in 1862 in which the 28th fell on a Monday. His reference to "the late news" from Tennessee that caused him to turn down business opportunities probably refers to the capture of Nashville by the Union Army on February 25th, followed by the fall of Memphis in June (Ash). [Back]

    8. Anderson is probably referring to the Mount de Chantal Visitation Academy, incorporated as the Wheeling Female Academy under the auspices of the Sisters of Visitation in 1848 ("May 31, 2008: Mount de Chantal Visitation Academy Graduates Last Class"). [Back]

    9.  Anderson probably meant to write "Kanawha," referring either to Kanawha City, WV on the Kanawha River, which flows into the Ohio at Point Pleasant, OH between Cincinnati and Parkersburg, or the Little Kanawha River, which flows into the Ohio at Parkersburg. [Back]

    10. Presumably, Anderson is referring to the Kentucky State Prison in Frankfort, south of Cincinnati. [Back]

    11. Anderson records the day as Sunday, but in August 1862, the 16th fell on a Saturday. [Back]

    12. June 19, 1863 fell on a Friday, not Saturday. [Back]

    13. A lot labeled "Holmes" appears at 15 Market Street on an 1872 map of Pittsburg. ( Historic Pittsburgh, https://historicpittsburgh.org/islandora/object/pitt%3A1872p015/viewer). [Back]

    14. Anderson's mention of the Battle of Lebanon places the date of the letter on Sunday, July 5, 1863. [Back]

    15. Sutlers goods were the everyday items such as food, tools, and clothing sold by peddlers to the soldiers in the war. [Back]

    16. During the Civil War, the Adams Express Company shipped goods and money to and from Union troops ("Adams Express Company Journal"). [Back]

    About this View of the Letters

    This presentation of the Anderson letters derives from the project's main XML file, anderson_letters.xml, transformed with anderson_common.xsl and anderson_reading.xsl. In this "view" of the Anderson letters, line breaks in the manuscripts are not reported; rather, lines wrap in the browser's window. Similarly, page breaks are not reported. Paragraph breaks follow those in the manuscript. Spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and abbreviations are reported as they appear in the manuscript. Text highlighted by the correspondents with an underscore is underscored in this view. Canceled text is suppressed. Text added between lines or in the margins is silently incorporated into the reading text. Uncertain readings are enclosed in square brackets and followed by a question mark, and text supplied by the editor for clarity is set in italics and surrounded by square brackets. Links to notes and external materials are superscripted and colored.
