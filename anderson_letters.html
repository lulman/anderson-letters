    Introduction: Anderson Letters Intro Title


    Salutation from letter of 27 June 1862

    In some ways, William B. Anderson's short letter of 27 June 1862 to his wife, Mary Louisa, typifies the twenty-one letters from William to Louisa that survive in the collection of the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library at The Ohio State University. It addresses three of the four broad topics about which he repeatedly writes to his wife during the summers of 1862, 1863, and 1864: managing domestic affairs and sustaining familial bonds when family members are separated for long periods by great distances; the rhetoric and logistics of mid-nineteenth-century correspondence; and the economic, logistical, and physical challenges of the riverboat business, especially on a waterway as critical as the Ohio River in the midst of the American Civil War (in other letters, Anderson also reflects more broadly on the uncertainties, vicissitudes, and suffering faced by civilians and soldiers alike in wartime).

    However, this letter is also unique. All but two of the letters were written from Cincinnati, a thriving center of commerce on the Ohio River approximately halfway between its source at the confluence of the Allegheny and Monongahela Rivers in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania and its confluence with the Mississippi River a mile or so south of Cairo, Illinois. The remaining letter in the collection was written from St. Louis, Missouri—though Anderson mentions writing from Louisville, Kentucky and Nashville, Tennessee as well. This letter was written from Pittsburgh, William and Louisa's home town. Presumably, he had been visiting his family and had expected to leave Pittsburgh by the Friday in late June when he wrote the letter; he writes to inform his wife of a delay.

    As the collection in general reveals, delays were common, caused variously by negotiations over freight, water levels on the river, mechanical difficulties, and various military complications. Arrangements regarding freight seem to have kept Anderson in Pittsburgh overnight on June 27. He wants to secure a full load before leaving Pittsburgh because he "cannot depend upon getting any freight below here." He takes the opportunity to ask Louisa to send down "the Can of milk and 2 jars of lard and my clothes and overcoat." The definite article stands out: the Can of milk. One wonders whether, in his habitual haste (on which he often blames poor spelling and handwriting), he has simply rushed off and forgotten these items, and Louisa has sent him a note asking what she should do with them. In any case, in his haste to send her instructions, he crosses out the next two lines of his letter and recomposes them. Characteristically, the life of a riverboat captain seems frenetic.

    It is also worth noting that he arranges to convey a note to his wife via "some one on the Shore at Shousetown." While the Andersons clearly handled much of their correspondence through the U.S. mail (he mentions post offices several times, and the collection includes one stamped envelope), they often convey both messages and goods through intermediaries traveling by rail or river.

    Anderson's closing request constitutes something of a refrain in the letters. He asks Louisa to call on a Mr. Russell (probably John Russell, a local merchant) in order to collect money owed him, noting that he must meet his obligations on the crew's upcoming payday. Aside from suggesting that riverboat captains had to watch their cash flow carefully, this request reminds us that Louisa played a key role not only in caring for the Anderson household, including their four children (a fifth would be born in 1865), but also in tending to their business interests. Though we have only William Anderson's side of the correspondence, the letters reveal many details of Louisa's everyday life as well.

    It is Louisa, presumably, whom we have to thank for initially preserving the letters, probably for the very common, personal reasons to which David Levy alludes in Scrolling Forward: Making Sense of Documents in the Digital Age: "In the privacy of our own homes we are likely to hold on to any number of treasures: cards, letters, and notes written long ago, photographs, scrapbooks filled with mementoes from an earlier era. We can't throw them out, even if we can't say why. We may look at them rarely or not at all, yet still it is somehow comforting to know they are there. It's as if something of importance is embedded in their very substance. This has less to do with their information content per se than with the emotions they evoke and the personal relationships they signify and embody" (96–97). The Rare Books and Manuscripts Library at The Ohio State University purchased the letters in part because they fit into a larger goal of acquiring materials that reveal the "common man's view of great events," and war letters are one focus of that larger effort (Smith). The collection as a whole, and the letter of 27 June 1862 more specifically, should also prove interesting to business historians and students of letter writing in American culture (Hewitt). The passing of time has made the everyday information in the letters newly valuable, though the letters also retain the power Levy speaks of to evoke the emotional fabric of everyday life, especially when it becomes frayed in the course of great events.

    Project Description

    This edition belongs to a series of collaboratively produced electronic editions of previously unpublished nineteenth-century American manuscripts held in the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library (RBMS) at The Ohio State University Libraries. Other editions include "Selected Entries from the Lucius Clark Smith Diaries, 30 July 1862 to 31 December 1862"; "My Dear Elizabeth," five letters written by Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to her sister Elizabeth Palmer Peabody between ca. 1837 and 1868; "Journal of a Tour to Europe," by Samuel Sullivan Cox; and Louisa A. Doane's "Journal of Two Ocean Voyages (1852-1853)."

    Each text was initially edited in connection with an undergraduate or graduate course on electronic textual editing offered by H. Lewis Ulman, Associate Professor of English at The Ohio State University. The courses provided students with an opportunity to explore issues of textuality and mediation that arise when our cultural archives spread from page to screen and from library shelves to networked databases. Electronic textual editing served as each class's context for examining what happens—and envisioning what might happen—when artifacts in one medium are represented in another medium, especially with regard to the interpretive work of reading. Electronic textual editions also provided a contact zone that helped students reflect on what manuscript, print, and "born digital" artifacts can tell us about their unique properties and their relationships to one another.

    While the students' work focused on providing reliable textual editions, they also explored four threads inextricably woven into the work of electronic textual editing: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts; the history, characteristics, and preservation of physical and digital texts; the editorial processes and decisions that shape particular interpretations and presentations texts; and the mediation of electronic delivery systems.

    These conceptual focuses, along with the time constraints of academic terms and the need to train students who had, typically, no prior experience in textual editing or the various technologies employed in electronic textual editing, strongly influenced the selection of texts in this series. Working with curators in the RBMS, Professor Ulman identified previously unpublished manuscript materials that were of potentially broad interest and that reflected a curatorial focus of the RBMS collection—for example, letters and personal diaries that connect private lives with larger historical contexts. Focusing on unique manuscript materials not, in all but one case, associated with other copies or later published versions also eliminated the need to compare multiple copies or states of the same work, perhaps held in other archives, an impractical task in the course of an academic term. Other practical matters also informed the choice of materials, including the possibility of scanning the source documents before the class so that students could work with both the original documents and high-resolutions facsimiles as they transcribed and encoded the source texts.

    Thus, the editions in this series are documentary editions—interpretations and representations of single documents—designed to provide wider exposure and access to manuscript materials that might otherwise be overlooked, and to provide users with a variety of tools for studying those texts.

    While providing a reliable textual edition is a sine qua non, the Stephens edition also explores ways of telling four stories inextricably woven into our work: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts, the history of the physical documents, the editorial process that gave rise to this particular interpretation of the texts, and the mediation of our electronic delivery system.

    Over the course of several years following the classes in which students worked on the editions, Professor Ulman revised the introductory materials to ensure stylistic consistency and proofed the transcription and encoding. He also redesigned the structure of the editions to meet the requirements of Ohio State's institutional repository, evolving accessibility standards, and feedback from peer reviewers. However, he did not undertake a complete retranscription of the source text or revision of the students' introductory essays and annotations.

    About the Source Documents

    Title: "Civil War correspondence, 1862-1864"
    Extent: "Includes 21 handwritten letters, 4 documents, and 1 CD-ROM. Letters written by William B. Anderson to his wife in Pennsylvania. He writes from his steamboat during the Civil War, as he ferries Union troops, ammunition and supplies up and down the river at Cincinnati and vicinity. Also contains documents showing his expenses and profits and a letter from another person. All letters are digitized and available on a CD, included in the collection" (OSU Libraries Catalog).

    See individual letters (under "Views of the Letters") for physical descriptions of each letter.

    Selection of the Source Text. This edition contains the twenty letters written by William B. Anderson to his wife, Louisa Anderson, taking a literal rather than figurative clue from what Anderson calls in one letter the "distres [sic] that this war has brought on the human family." By contrast, the additional letter in the collection and the four receipts deal exclusively with Anderson's shipping business.

    The W. B. Anderson Collection is the property of the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library of The Ohio State University Libraries. Any use of the material should acknowledge such ownership.

    Editorial Method

    Scanning. The manuscript pages were scanned as 24-bit TIFF images at 400 dots per inch (dpi), then coverted to 8-bit JPEG images at 400 dpi and full quality. All edges were overscanned by at least 1/4 inch. No color correction was used at any stage of image preparation, but a scan of a photographer's 18% grey card on the same scanner produced RGB values at the center of 91/95/107 after applying a 150% Gaussian Blur in Adobe Photoshop to average color values. Thus, a properly configured monitor should provide a reasonable approximation of colors on the manuscript pages..

    Transcription. Editing of the Anderson letters emphasizes diplomatic transcription: abbreviations, recoverable cancelations, punctuation, spelling, underlining, additions above and below the line or in the margins, capitalization, line breaks, and page breaks have all been transcribed and encoded as they appear in the original manuscripts. The representation of those features varies depending on the view of the letter. An explanation of the presentation in each view of the letters can be read via the "About this View" link at the top of each view's page.

    Markup and Annotation. XML encoding of the Anderson letters follows The Text Encoding Initiative's (TEI) P5: Guidelines for Electronic Text Encoding and Interchange, as customized for the project using the TEI's ODD ("One Document Does it All") process. This editorial introduction and the three "views" of the letters were generated from the main XML file using XSL transformations to produce HTML Web pages. Several of the appendices were encoded in HTML separately. A more detailed description of the markup can be found via the "Appendices" menu, and all source files (XML, XSL, CSS, ODD, RNC) can be viewed and downloaded via the "Source Files" menu.

    Introductory material (such as this editorial introduction) as well as annotated lists of people, places, ships, and organizations mentioned in the letters are encoded in the TEI header of the main XML document. Other explanatory annotations are linked directly from the text. Separate appendices provide further information about the project (e.g., acknowledgements, editors' bios, and so on). All these ancillary materials can be viewed via the menus at the top of each page.

    Proofing. Students who created the original transcriptions proofread their own transcription and encoding. In preparation for peer review by NINES and preservation in The Ohio State University's Knowledge Bank, the transcriptions and encoding were once again proofread by Professor Ulman.

    Peer Review. Anonymous reviewers for NINES provided peer-reviews for the editions, in response to which they were revised and then reviewed by NINES editors before being listed in the NINES database.

    Production and Archival Location of Digital Assets. All of the files associated with this edition are accessed via, and archived in, The Ohio State University's institutional repository, the Knowledge Bank.

    List of People Mentioned

    Anderson, John L. (b. 1859; d. 1922). John L. Anderson (1859-1922). Son of William B. Anderson and Mary Louisa Fischgens Anderson. Resided at the homestead. Wounded in Spottsylvania, Va., May 14, 1864, while serving the sixty-second Pennsylvania volunteers from Antietam to Appomattox. He survived typhoid in 1890. He never married. He is buried in the Allegheny Cemetery.

    Anderson, Mary Louisa, née Fischgens (b. 1837; d. 1911). Mary Louisa Fischgens (1837-1911) and Capt. William B. Anderson (1822-1897) were married September 15, 1853 in Cincinnati, Ohio. They had five children.

    Anderson, William B. (b. 1822; d. 1897).

    Anderson, William Y. (b. 1855; d. 1890). Son of William B. Anderson and Mary Louisa Fischgens Anderson. He Married Nancy C. McKinely. He resided in Shoustown, Pa. He died of typhoid in PA, leaving a widow and two children. He is buried in Allegheny Cemetery. He left a widow and two young children.

    Fischgens, Anna Mary, née Woolslayer (b. 1812-07-12; d. 1897-11-25). Mary Louisa Anderson's mother.

    George Hatch. George Hatch served as mayor of Cincinnati from 1861-1863.

    Morgan, John Hunt (b. 1825-06-01; d. 1864-09-04). In his letter of 5 July 1863, Anderson refers to the burning of Lebanon, KY during one of General Morgan's raids on Union forces.

    Russel, John. John Russel was “a prominent merchant on Liberty Street for many years.”

    Wright, Horatio Gouverneur (b. 1820-03-06; d. 1899-07-02). General Wright commanded the Division of Ohio from August 1862 to March 1863.

    List of Organizations Mentioned

    Adams Express Company: A shipping company that operated through the South, Southwest, and North.

    Anderson Ferry: Ferry service across the Ohio River from Cincinnati, OH to Covington, KY. [NEED CITATION]

    Little Miami Rairoad: The Little Miami Railroad was only the second railroad to be built in Ohio. The state legislature granted the Little Miami Railroad Company a charter in March 1836. The purpose was to connect the city of Cincinnati to Springfield. The line was completed between 1837 and 1848

    List of Places Mentioned in the Letters

    Allegheny City. United States; Pennsylvania Allegheny (Lat/Long: 40.451029 -80.015539).

    Augusta, Kentucky. United States; Kentucky Bracken (Lat/Long: 38.7667 -84.0000). A city in Kentucky along the Ohio River east of Cincinnati, Ohio. In his letter of 28 September 1862, Anderson reports that he has been ordered to take soldiers to Augusta in response to the Battle of Augusta, which took place the previous day.

    Blennerhassett Island. United States; West Virgina Wood (Lat/Long: 39.2667 -81.6167). In his letter of 10 August 1862, Anderson describes having to lighten his ship's load to navigate around Blennerhassett Island, near Belpre, Ohio

    Buffington Island. United States; Ohio Meigs (Lat/Long: 38.9833 -81.7667). In his letter of 10 August 1862, Anderson describes having to lighten his ship's load to navigate around Buffington Island, an island on the Ohio River in Jackson County, West Virginia north of Pomeroy, Ohio. Nearby in Ohio, the only battle to occur on Ohio soil during the American Civil War, the Battle of Buffington Island, took place on July 19, 1863.

    Camp Portsmouth. United States; Ohio Scioto (Lat/Long: 38.7167 -82.9833). Camp Portsmouth served as a training and organizational camp from 1861-1862 for The 117th Regiment Ohio Volunteer Infantry, the 1st Regiment Ohio Heavy Artillery, and the 18th Regiment Ohio Light Artillery "Camp Portsmouth".

    Cincinnati, OH. United States; Ohio Hamilton (Lat/Long: 39.1500 -84.4500).

    Evansville. United States; Indiana Vanderburgh (Lat/Long: 37.9667 -87.5500). Located on the Ohio River opposite Henderson, Kentucky.

    Gallipolis. United States; Ohio Gallia (Lat/Long: 38.7833 -82.2000). Gallipolis is a city along the Ohio River in Gallia County, Southeast Ohio

    Graham's Station. United States; Ohio Meigs (Lat/Long: 38.9667 -81.9000). Graham's Station, Ohio was incorporated in 1841 and was later renamed Racine.

    Lebanon. United States; Kentucky Marion (Lat/Long: 37.5667 -85.2500).

    Lexington. United States; Kentucky Fayette (Lat/Long: 38.0333 -84.5000). In his letter of 5 July 1863, Anderson notes that General Morgan's forces were likely to take Lexington, but in fact they did not.

    Licking River. United States; Kentucky Morgan (Lat/Long: 37.9333 -83.3000). In his letter of 5 July 1863, Anderson mentions navigating a mile up the Licking River to deliver a load of metal.

    Louisville. United States; Kentucky Jefferson (Lat/Long: 38.2500 -85.7500). In his letter of June 16, Anderson notes that he did not write to Louisa during a previous stop in Louisville, KY.

    Maysville. United States; (Lat/Long: 38.6333 -83.7333).

    Memphis, TN. United States; Tennessee Shelby (Lat/Long: 35.1333 -90.0333). Ship Kenton in Memphis trade.

    Nashville. United States; Tennessee Davidson (Lat/Long: 36.1540 -86.7610 ). In his letter of 1 July 1863, Anderson he will start for Nashville, Tennessee that evening. Nashville could be reached from the Ohio River via the Cumberland River, which meets the Ohio at mile 933.

    Parkersburg, WV. United States; West Virginia Wood (Lat/Long: 39.2667 -81.5500). In his Letter of 24 August 1864, Anderson speculates about whether water levels will allow him to navigate as far upstream as Parkersburg, WV.
